Time Value of Money (TVM)

💡 Intuition

Money today is worth more than the same amount in the future due to its earning potential. This fundamental concept underlies all financial decision-making. Think of it as the "opportunity cost" of money - by having money today, you can invest it and earn returns.

Key Formulas

Future Value (FV)

FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ

  • PV = Present Value
  • r = Interest rate per period
  • n = Number of periods

Present Value (PV)

PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ

This is simply the inverse of the FV formula - discounting future cash flows back to today.

Annuity Present Value

PVA = PMT × [1 - (1 + r)⁻ⁿ] / r

  • PMT = Payment per period
  • Used for: Mortgages, bonds, regular payments

Practical Applications

Example: Investment Decision

Scenario: You can invest $10,000 today at 8% annual interest for 5 years.

Calculation: FV = $10,000 × (1.08)⁵ = $14,693.28

Insight: Your investment will grow by $4,693.28 over 5 years.

🎯 CFA Exam Tips

  • Always identify the cash flow pattern first
  • Draw a timeline for complex problems
  • Remember: ordinary annuity vs. annuity due
  • Practice with your calculator extensively

Descriptive Statistics & Probability

💡 Intuition

Statistics help us understand data patterns and make informed decisions under uncertainty. In finance, we use these tools to measure risk, return, and the likelihood of various outcomes. Think of statistics as your "data detective" tools.

Measures of Central Tendency

Arithmetic Mean

X̄ = Σxᵢ / n

Use: Average return, typical value

Limitation: Sensitive to outliers

Geometric Mean

G = (x₁ × x₂ × ... × xₙ)^(1/n)

Use: Average growth rates, compound returns

Key insight: Always less than arithmetic mean (except when all values are equal)

Measures of Dispersion

Variance

σ² = Σ(xᵢ - μ)² / n

Sample Variance: s² = Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² / (n-1)

Standard Deviation

σ = √σ²

Interpretation: Average deviation from the mean

Risk measure: Higher σ = higher volatility/risk

Probability Concepts

Expected Value

E(X) = Σ[P(xᵢ) × xᵢ]

Weighted average of all possible outcomes

Example: Portfolio Return Analysis

Stock Returns: 5%, 12%, -3%, 8%, 15%

Arithmetic Mean: (5+12-3+8+15)/5 = 7.4%

Standard Deviation: √[(5-7.4)² + (12-7.4)² + ... ] / 4 = 6.77%

Interpretation: Expected return of 7.4% with 6.77% volatility

Statistical Distributions

💡 Intuition

Distributions describe how data points are spread out. In finance, they help us model uncertainty and calculate probabilities. The normal distribution is the "gold standard" - many financial variables follow this bell-shaped pattern.

Normal Distribution

Standardization Formula

Z = (X - μ) / σ

  • Z = Standard normal variable
  • X = Original variable
  • μ = Population mean
  • σ = Population standard deviation

Key Properties of Normal Distribution

  • Symmetric around the mean
  • 68% of data within 1 standard deviation
  • 95% of data within 2 standard deviations
  • 99.7% of data within 3 standard deviations

Other Important Distributions

Student's t-Distribution

When to use: Small samples (n < 30)

Key feature: Wider tails than normal

Degrees of freedom: n - 1

Chi-Square Distribution

When to use: Variance testing

Key feature: Always positive

Degrees of freedom: n - 1

Example: Risk Assessment

Scenario: Stock returns are normally distributed with μ = 10%, σ = 15%

Question: What's the probability of returns being negative?

Solution: Z = (0 - 10) / 15 = -0.67

Result: P(Z < -0.67) = 25.14% chance of negative returns

Modern Portfolio Theory

💡 Intuition

The famous "don't put all your eggs in one basket" principle, but with mathematical precision. By combining assets that don't move perfectly together, you can reduce risk without sacrificing expected return. It's about finding the optimal risk-return trade-off.

Portfolio Return & Risk

Portfolio Expected Return

E(Rₚ) = Σ wᵢ × E(Rᵢ)

  • wᵢ = Weight of asset i
  • E(Rᵢ) = Expected return of asset i
  • Note: Σwᵢ = 1 (weights sum to 100%)

Portfolio Variance (2 assets)

σₚ² = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂σ₁σ₂ρ₁₂

  • ρ₁₂ = Correlation coefficient between assets 1 and 2
  • Key insight: Correlation determines diversification benefit

Correlation & Covariance

Correlation Coefficient

ρ = Cov(X,Y) / (σₓ × σᵧ)

Range: -1 ≤ ρ ≤ +1

  • ρ = +1: Perfect positive correlation
  • ρ = 0: No linear relationship
  • ρ = -1: Perfect negative correlation

Diversification Benefits

  • ρ < 1: Diversification reduces risk
  • Lower correlation = Greater diversification benefit
  • Optimal: Mix of low-correlated assets
  • Limit: Cannot eliminate systematic risk

Example: Two-Asset Portfolio

Asset A: E(R) = 12%, σ = 20%

Asset B: E(R) = 8%, σ = 15%

Correlation: ρ = 0.3

Weights: 60% A, 40% B

Portfolio Return: 0.6×12% + 0.4×8% = 10.4%

Portfolio Risk: √[0.6²×20² + 0.4²×15² + 2×0.6×0.4×20×15×0.3] = 16.55%

Hypothesis Testing

💡 Intuition

Hypothesis testing is like being a detective with data. You start with a claim (hypothesis), gather evidence (sample data), and decide whether the evidence is strong enough to challenge your initial belief. It's a structured way to make decisions under uncertainty.

⚠️ Critical Terminology Note

We NEVER "accept" a hypothesis in statistical testing!

  • Correct: "Reject H₀" or "Fail to reject H₀"
  • Incorrect: "Accept H₀" or "Accept H₁"
  • Why: We can only determine if evidence contradicts our assumption, not prove it's true

Hypothesis Testing Framework

Step-by-Step Process

  1. State Hypotheses: H₀ (null) and H₁ (alternative)
  2. Select Significance Level: α (typically 0.05)
  3. Calculate Test Statistic: t, z, etc.
  4. Determine Critical Value(s)
  5. Make Decision: Reject or fail to reject H₀
  6. State Conclusion in context

Common Test Statistics

Z-Test (Known σ)

z = (x̄ - μ₀) / (σ/√n)

Use when: Large sample OR known population σ

t-Test (Unknown σ)

t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n)

Use when: Small sample AND unknown σ

df: n - 1

Types of Errors

Type I Error (α)

Definition: Rejecting H₀ when it's true

Probability: Significance level (α)

Consequence: False positive

Type II Error (β)

Definition: Failing to reject H₀ when it's false

Power: 1 - β (probability of correctly rejecting false H₀)

Consequence: False negative

Why We Don't "Accept" Hypotheses

Philosophical Reason: We can never prove a hypothesis is definitively true with sample data.

Statistical Reason: "Failing to reject" ≠ "Accepting" - lack of evidence against H₀ doesn't prove H₀ is correct.

Analogy: In court, "not guilty" doesn't mean "innocent" - it means insufficient evidence for conviction.

Example: Testing Portfolio Performance

Claim: Fund manager achieves returns > 10% annually

H₀: μ ≤ 10% (null hypothesis)

H₁: μ > 10% (alternative hypothesis)

Sample: n = 25, x̄ = 12%, s = 5%, α = 0.05

Test statistic: t = (12 - 10) / (5/√25) = 2.0

Critical value: t₀.₀₅,₂₄ = 1.711

Decision: Since 2.0 > 1.711, we reject H₀

Conclusion: Evidence supports that returns exceed 10%

Regression & Correlation

💡 Intuition

Regression is like finding the "best-fit line" through scattered data points. It helps us understand relationships between variables and make predictions. In finance, we use it to model how stock prices relate to market movements, or how economic factors affect investment returns.

Simple Linear Regression

Regression Equation

Y = a + bX + ε

  • Y = Dependent variable
  • X = Independent variable
  • a = Intercept (Y when X = 0)
  • b = Slope (change in Y per unit change in X)
  • ε = Error term

Slope Coefficient

b = Cov(X,Y) / Var(X)

Alternative formula: b = r × (sᵧ/sₓ)

Interpretation: For each 1-unit increase in X, Y changes by b units

Coefficient of Determination (R²)

R² = (Correlation)² = r²

Interpretation: Percentage of Y's variation explained by X

Range: 0 ≤ R² ≤ 1

ANOVA Table

Source Sum of Squares df Mean Square
Regression RSS 1 MSR = RSS/1
Error SSE n-2 MSE = SSE/(n-2)
Total TSS n-1 -

F-statistic: F = MSR / MSE

Regression Assumptions

Key Assumptions (Remember: LINE)

  • Linearity: Relationship between X and Y is linear
  • Independence: Observations are independent
  • Normality: Residuals are normally distributed
  • Equal variance: Constant variance of residuals (homoscedasticity)

Example: Stock Beta Analysis

Model: R_stock = α + β × R_market + ε

Data: Monthly returns for 36 months

Results: α = 0.02, β = 1.25, R² = 0.64

Interpretation:

  • Stock has 2% monthly alpha (excess return)
  • Stock is 25% more volatile than market (β = 1.25)
  • Market explains 64% of stock's return variation

🎯 CFA Exam Tips - Regression

  • Always check R² for explanatory power
  • Correlation doesn't imply causation
  • Watch for heteroscedasticity in residual plots
  • Know when to use t-test vs F-test
  • Understand confidence vs prediction intervals
